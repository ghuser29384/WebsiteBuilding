Philosophical Foundations

Public Discourse in CHSEB

CHSEB treats ethical reasoning as a civic practice: students learn to disagree without dehumanizing.

Two Foundational Lenses

Michael Sandel: Civic Moral Inquiry

Sandel emphasizes that public life cannot avoid moral questions. In CHSEB, teams examine competing conceptions of the good and justify them in shared civic language.

Jurgen Habermas: Discourse Ethics

Habermas argues that legitimacy grows from inclusive, reason-giving dialogue. CHSEB rounds are designed so claims must survive public scrutiny, not mere rhetorical force.

Additional Theories in Public Discourse

Rawls: Public Reason and Reciprocity

In constitutional-level disagreements, students ask whether reasons are shareable across reasonable pluralism.

Arendt: Action, Plurality, and the Public Realm

Public judgment emerges when distinct persons appear together, speak, and take responsibility for common worlds.

Mouffe: Agonistic Democracy

Conflict cannot be eliminated; the task is to transform enemies into adversaries within shared democratic rules.

Fricker: Epistemic Injustice

CHSEB trains students to detect credibility gaps and hermeneutical exclusion in who gets heard and understood.

Mill: Free Speech and Error-Correction

Even unpopular claims can sharpen truth-tracking, provided discourse norms protect participants from abuse.

Confucian Debates: Harmony, Remonstrance, and Role Ethics

Students examine when social harmony supports dialogue and when moral remonstrance requires direct criticism.

Major Debates in Public Discourse

Civility vs. Urgency

Should discourse prioritize calm civility, or can urgent injustice justify disruptive forms of argument?

Neutrality vs. Moral Perfectionism

Must institutions remain neutral among conceptions of the good, or should they cultivate substantive civic virtues?

Deliberation vs. Contestation

Is public reason primarily consensus-seeking deliberation, or an enduring field of principled contest?

Open Platforms vs. Harm Reduction

How should speech spaces balance openness with responsibilities to prevent harassment, disinformation, and silencing?

Norms for Ethical Public Dialogue

Reciprocity

Give reasons your counterparts could reasonably engage, not slogans only your side can endorse.

Charitable Interpretation

State your counterpart's argument fairly before criticizing it.

Evidence and Principles

Connect examples to ethical principles and avoid unsupported assertion.

Revisability

Signal what evidence or reasoning would change your position.

What Students Practice

  • Separating disagreement from disrespect.
  • Balancing rights, welfare, and fairness in policy dilemmas.
  • Translating private convictions into public reasons.
  • Building common ground without erasing real conflict.

Suggested Dialogue Protocol

  1. Clarify the ethical question before taking positions.
  2. Name key stakeholders and what each is owed.
  3. Test arguments against counterexamples and edge cases.
  4. Close with provisional judgments and open questions.
